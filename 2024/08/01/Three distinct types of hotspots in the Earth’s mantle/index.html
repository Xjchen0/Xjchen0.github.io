> Abstract

Background

  • The origin of mantle hotspots

  • only seven (‘primary’) out of 49 hotspots --> a very deep origin

  • in each hemisphere these move slowly

Scientific Question(s) or Phenomena

  • This correlates with latitudinal shifts in the Hawaiian and Reunion hotspots

  • with a change in true polar wander

Conclusion

> Five possible characteristics of a deep plume

  • Temperature-dependent viscosity fluid --> mushroom-shaped head and a thin, long stem -->这里是说地幔柱下部细长，在转换带或以上类似蘑菇头，有学者推测它跟海底正地形有关

    • (2) traps at their initiation

  • Plumes coming from the bottom of the mantle with a buoyancy flux of less than 103kg⋅s−110^3 kg\cdot s^{-1}103kg⋅s−1 would not melt beneath old lithosphere and would be sheared by mantle flow before reaching the lithosphere

    • (3) a flux value in excess of 103kg⋅s−110^3kg\cdot s^{-1}103kg⋅s−1 as a minimum for a ‘prominent’ hotspot

  • The distribution of rare gas isotopic ratios in volcanic rocks has been shown to discriminate well mid-ocean ridge basalt (MORB) from ocean island basalt (OIB) sources. High 4He/3He^4 He/^3 He4He/3He or 21Ne/22Ne^{21} Ne/^{22} Ne21Ne/22Ne ratios of hotspot lavas have often been attributed to upwellings from a long-isolated and more primitive reservoir.

    • Alle’gre recently estimated that the depleted mantle reservoir corresponds to 40% of the total mantle, implying the existence of an exchange between the upper and lower mantle through the 670 km discontinuity, and a possible discontinuity at 820 km depth.
    • (4) A high He or Ne ratio

  • Hotspots are, by definition, hot --> anomalously low shear velocities (VSV_SVS​)

> Different types of hotspots on Earth

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=5&selection=46,3,46,38|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 5]]

Election of the ‘primary’ plumes

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=5&selection=48,6,48,38|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 5]]

  • retain only seven hotspots as qualifying candidates for deepest, primary plumes: they are Hawaii, Easter and Louisville in the Pacific hemisphere and Iceland, Afar, Reunion and Tristan in the Indo-Atlantic hemisphere.

    • only the nine hotspots meet at least three out of the five possible criteria
    • One of the nine hotspots with a count of at least three, Samoa, displays a clear, short track without a flood basalt or oceanic plateau at the onset. Whereas absence of evidence (such as is the case for Hawaii, where an original flood basalt may have been subducted) does not allow us to eliminate a potential candidate for primary hotspot, evidence of absence of a starting plume head is taken as evidence that the corresponding hotspot is not of the same type. Caroline seems to have no tomographic anomaly nor an associated flood basalt(which is the most important?)

A fixed hotspot reference frame?

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=6&selection=10,5,10,36|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 6]]

  • Whether they represent a fixed reference frame

    • Hotspots with different origins have been (erroneously) combined
      • Average velocities for the last 65 Ma between the Hawaiian hotspot and those in the Indo-Atlantic hemisphere have been 10~20 mm/a
      • Motions between certain Pacific hotspots must have reached at least 60 mm/a
    • When one restricts the analysis to the three primary hotspot candidates, there is no evidence for inter-hotspot motion significantly larger than 5 mm/a --> less than rms plate velocities, are to first order ‘small’
    • Clearly, the analysis of the large kinked seamount tracks left by Hawaii, Easter and Louisville, originally made by Morgan, remains valid to first order
    • Hotspots indeed provide a quasi-fixed frame in each hemisphere over the last 80~100 Ma

  • If there was any motion between the two hotspot ensembles.

    • The importance of an extinct plate boundary within the Adare trough in Antarctica我们若想要知道太平洋热点与南非热点之间的相对运动，可以借助热点和板块之间的相对运动（这个可以通过热点轨迹得知）和板块和板块之间的相对运动（如果板块不相邻，那么我们就得找一个共同的参照物），对于太平洋板块和非洲板块，Adare海沟是一个极好的参照物
    • Reunion and Hawaii have remained fixed with respect to each other(hyp.) -->
      • The location of the Hawaiian hotspot back in time
      • The two hotspots have actually drifted slowly, at ~10 mm/a, for the last 45 Ma, but at a much faster rate (~50 mm/a) prior to that
    • We conclude that the primary hotspots form two distinct subsets in each one of the two geodynamically distinct hemispheres. Each subset deforms an order of magnitude slower than typical plate velocities. The two subsets have been in slow motion for the last 45 Ma, but in much faster motion in the previous (at least 35 Ma long) period.

Plumes and superplumes

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=8&selection=112,5,112,27|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 8]]

  • The two hemispheres also correspond to the dominant degree 2 observed in the geoid.the dominant degree 2说的是两极式的异常，与quadrupolar convection相对应

  • Present-day convection in the lower mantle appears to be dominated by a quadrupolar mode

  • Many hotspots are located above these ‘hot’ regions (Fig. 1b).

  • The pattern in the hot hemispheres may be more complex

    • not only hotter but likely chemically heterogeneous

  • Six out of seven of our primary hotspots are found at their margins. Only the Icelandic plume is rather remote from them.

  • The dynamic swells associated with the superplumes and at least five of our primary plumes (Louisville, Hawaii, Tristan, Reunion and Iceland) do not overlap --> they may not originate from the superplumes

Origin of primary plumes

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=8&selection=232,5,232,29|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 8]]

  • Primary hotspots can be traced in the upper mantle down to the transition zone

    • The most likely locations of such boundary layers are in the transition zone and at the core-mantle boundary
    • Seismology is as yet unable to resolve the stems of individual plumes in the lower mantle and therefore cannot tell if the primary plumes come from deeper than the transition zone
    • A recent geochemical analysis advocates such a transition zone origin
    • The fact that lavas from flood basalts may be significantly enriched in Fe with respect to OIB (E. Humler, personal communication, 2002) would then favor a lower mantle origin for primary plumes

Two types of upwelling in the lower mantle and two types of hotspots

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=9&selection=119,5,120,25|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 9]]

  • The question now is whether two scales of upwelling, ‘superplumes’ and ‘primary’ plumes, can both originate from the bottom of the lower mantle.

    • Recent experiments by Davaille et al. show that simultaneous generation of superplumes and hotspot plumes indeed arises naturally from thermochemical convection in a heterogeneous mantle

  • Its style depends on a local buoyancy ratio (ratio of the chemical density anomaly to the thermal density anomaly)

    • low buoyancy ratio --> large domes or ‘superplumes’
    • higher buoyancy ratio --> long-lived thermochemical plume

  • Density anomalies of chemical origin in the Earth’s mantle inferred from seismic data and mineral physics studies (probably less than 2%) would be sufficient to produce both modes.

    • Moreover, since a thermal boundary layer would exist at the interface between a superplume and the rest of the mantle, it could generate sec- ondary thermal plumes, all the more if the dome is stuck at the transition zone
    • Note that surface motions of these sec- ondary plumes could also re£ect lower mantle convection, hence be consistent with those derived for primary plumes
    • The associated tracks are in most cases too short for a signi¢cant test of this

Tracing convection in the lower mantle

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=11&selection=70,6,70,42|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 11]]

  • The primary (and probably also the secondary) plumes behave as quasi-passive tracers of the large-scale motion imposed on the lower mantle by quadrupolar convection

  • The two subsets of primary hotspots indicate that the two separate reservoirs of quadrupolar convection, centered on the Pacific and African super-upwellings, have moved little (~10 mm/a) in the last ~40-50 Ma with respect to each other, but underwent significant (~50 mm/a) rather uniform relative motion in the previous tens of millions of years.

  • We turn to TPW curves to estimate when this motion may have started.

  • Pre-100 Ma TPW estimates should be regarded with caution

  • What could have triggered TPW ~130 Ma

    • The geometry of density anomalies associated with upwellings (notably with the two superswells) does not have a large effect on the principal axes of inertia of the Earth and hence on TPW.
    • Cold subducted material, accumulated at the base of subduction zones, may trigger a major avalanche in the lower mantle.
    • An alternate interpretation would be the disappearance of a major subduction zone system, after which both heat flow and mean temperature would have been rapidly and significantly altered

  • The more recent event at 40-50 Ma could then be related to the closure of the huge Tethys subduction zone, following the generalization of Indian collision, as has been suggested for a long time in order to interpret the Hawaii-Emperor bend.

> Conclusions

[[Courtillot_2003_EPSL.pdf#page=11&selection=198,3,198,14|Courtillot_2003_EPSL, page 11]]

  • Surface hotspots on Earth may have three distinct origins

    • (a) At least seven would originate from the deepest part of the lower mantle (we might call them ‘Morganian’), probably anchored on chemical heterogeneities deposited in the D’’ layer. Because we made a conservative count, the actual number of primary hotspots could be higher, maybe on the order of 10.
    • (b) Some (~20) may originate from the bottom of the transition zone at the top of the large transient domes that correspond to the superswells: Caroline, Mc Donald, Pitcairn, Samoa and Tahiti are good candidates for these secondary hotspots.
    • (c) The remainder (~20) could be upper mantle features, and in that sense ‘Andersonian’. These hotspots may be linked to the asthenosphere and be a passive response to forms of lithospheric breakup.

  • Mixing the three distinct types of hotspots, with the hope of establishing a single origin, could be the reason for most of the debates that have opposed apparently conflicting, endmember models for the last decades.
